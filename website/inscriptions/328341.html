This inscription from Kos (ca. 200-150 BC) is a detailed 'diagraphÄ“' concerning the sale of the priesthood of Hermes. It is a classic example of a Hellenistic sacred law where the state commodifies a religious office. The text outlines the eligibility criteria for the purchaser (must be healthy and whole-limbed), the duration of the office (for life), and extensive privileges (ateleia), including exemption from military service and various liturgies like the trierarchy and lampadarchy. It also specifies the priest's ritual honors, such as proedria (front-row seating) at games and specific portions of sacrificed animals (skin and leg). The mention of the Hermaia festival and the involvement of the gymnasiarch and agonothetes highlights the close link between the cult of Hermes and the ephebic/gymnastic life of the Koan youth.
