This inscription from the island of Kos, dated to the early 2nd century BC (likely 196/195 BC), is a multifaceted document. It primarily functions as a sacred law and decree regulating the sale and administration of the priesthood (hierosyne) of Aphrodite Pandamos and Aphrodite Pontia. A significant historical detail is the mention of an earthquake ('seismon') that caused structural damage to the sanctuary's dining rooms (hestiatoria) and other facilities, prompting the need for repairs. The text outlines financial procedures for the priesthood's purchase, sacrificial obligations for various social groups (citizens, metics, and merchants), and the annual opening of the temple treasury. The document concludes with an extensive subscription list (epidosis) of women who contributed funds for the 'peribolos' (enclosure) and other architectural additions, providing valuable prosopographical data for Hellenistic Kos.
