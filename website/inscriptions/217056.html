This inscription, found at the Temple of Kalabsha (Talmis) in Nubia, is a complex poetic hymn (proskynema) composed by a Roman decurion named Maximus. Dating from the late 1st to the 3rd century AD, the text is written in a mixture of hexameters and iambics, showcasing the high level of Greek literary culture among Roman military officers stationed on the frontier. The poem recounts a divine vision (oneiros) where the Nubian sun-god Mandulis and the goddess Isis appear to the author. It is a significant example of religious syncretism, as it identifies Mandulis with the Greek Apollo and uses traditional Greek poetic forms to express devotion to local deities. The text is signed by Maximus, marking it as both a personal religious testimony and a public display of his literary and cultural identity.
