This inscription, dated to 95 BC during the reign of Ptolemy X Alexander I, records a petition from two men named Rhodes to the royal couple. They complain about the harassment of the temple of Heron in Magdola by local officials who were illegally demanding taxes and requisitions from the sacred land. The king grants the temple the right of asylia (asylum/inviolability) and orders the strategos Lysanias to ensure no one enters the temple forcibly or disturbs the priests. The document is a classic example of the Ptolemaic administrative process, involving a petition (enteuxis), a royal subscription (hypographe), and subsequent orders to provincial officials. It also highlights the bilingual nature of public notices in Hellenistic Egypt, as it mandates the erection of stelae in both Greek and 'native' (Demotic) characters.
