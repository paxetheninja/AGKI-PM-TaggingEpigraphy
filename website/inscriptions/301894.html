This 2nd-century BC decree from Pergamon establishes the hereditary priesthood of Asclepius for Asklepiades, son of Archas. It is a significant document for understanding the intersection of civic administration and religious cults in the Hellenistic period. The decree specifies the priest's rights, including wearing a crown, receiving specific portions of sacrificed animals (the right leg and skins), and enjoying tax exemption and proedria. The requirement to set up stelae in Pergamon (Asklepieion and Acropolis) and Mytilene highlights the regional importance of the Pergamene Asklepieion and the diplomatic ties between these cities. The text also mentions an oath to be taken at the altar of Zeus Soter to ensure the permanence of these honors.
