AI Analysis Summary

This inscription, known as the 'Currency Law of Pergamon' or the 'Edict on the Money-Changers,' dates to the reign of Hadrian (c. 129 AD). It records a decision by a high Roman official (likely the Proconsul of Asia) resolving a dispute between the city's small-scale traders (fishmongers, etc.) and the tax-farmers (misthotai) who held the monopoly on money-changing. The text regulates the exchange rate, fixing it at 18 assaria per denarius for small transactions to prevent the tax-farmers from overcharging. It also details the legal process for distraint (enchyrasia), involving the city's treasurers (tamiai) and former magistrates (strategoi), to ensure that seizures of property for debt were handled legally and through public slaves.
